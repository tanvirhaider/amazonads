The Power of Brands to Drive Social Change
How to share values-driven stories and reach new audiences.

Every marketer knows their brand is much more than a logo and a pithy tagline. And this was never clearer than coming out of 2020, when consumers were increasingly turning to brands to take action on key issues such as climate change, societal contributions, responsible business practices and more. Now is the time to speak to your brand values — and mean it.

Amazon Ads recently commissioned a survey from Environics Research about the values that matter most to consumers and found that 83 percent of U.S. respondents felt more brands should do their part in helping the world, especially during difficult times.

“The current generation of consumers expects brands to be values-driven and advocate for social good,” says Vann Graves, the executive director of Brandcenter at Virginia Commonwealth University. “To reach consumers, brands must be aware of and take part in the larger societal conversations happening in real time.”

Looking for inspiration? Here, we will explore Indeed’s Rising Voices program, which brought multiple brands together to give opportunities to underrepresented filmmakers. It is a powerful example of values-driven advertising in action.

The idea for Rising Voices grew from the concept that talent is universal but opportunity is not. And Indeed wanted to amplify the voices of artists who were BIPOC — Black, Indigenous or other people of color. “We thought, ‘What if, instead of spending $1 million on a big TV ad, we invested $1 million for 10 BIPOC creators to produce short films about the meaning of work?’” recalls LaFawn Davis, the senior vice president of environmental, social and governance at Indeed, the world’s No. 1 job site. “‘How would their unique perspective on work and life show up in these stories?’”

The Amazon Ads survey found that 72 percent of U.S. consumers want to see more diversity and representation in advertising. And 75 percent desire to learn more about the customs and heritage of racial and ethnic groups in their country. “In today’s competitive business climate, brands that choose not to include diverse voices may lose credibility among consumers,” according to Graves.

In early 2021, Indeed approached Emmy Award-winning artist Lena Waithe and Hillman Grad Productions, which aims to provide support systems for emerging filmmakers from underrepresented communities. Together, they honed the Rising Voices concept: They would give a budget of $100,000 to chosen filmmakers and collaborate with each of them to create a short film touching on the theme of the meaning of work. The films would be screened at the Tribeca Film Festival, which was just five months away.

Hillman Grad and Indeed announced the contest on social media and picked 11 filmmakers from over 800 scripts that were submitted, to create 10 shorts in only six weeks. A married couple, Deondray Gossfield and Quincy LeNear Gossfield, entered with a script for their powerful short film “Flames,” about former high school friends who found themselves at a turning point in their lives and relationship. “Deondray and I have been producing television together for many years,” says LeNear Gossfield. “Rising Voices is helping us make the tough transition to professional studio directing, which is what we really love.”

Indeed’s Rising Voices program took a stand on inclusivity, provided genuine support and mentorship to BIPOC filmmakers and had the potential to put meaningful stories from underrepresented populations in front of new audiences. But how could they reach a large number of viewers all around the country?

Indeed reached out to Amazon Ads to help.

Indeed and Hillman Grad had set in motion a fantastic program, but to fulfill their goals, they needed a space to amplify the talent of the filmmakers to a large audience. That’s why they turned to Amazon Ads, which offered them a wide audience through Fire TV, where viewers stream billions of hours every month. And with Fire TV’s Stream It Forward program, Amazon helped the campaign drive even greater impact by raising money for Goodwill, the chosen charity of Indeed, based on hours streamed.

“Ads on Fire TV capture customers’ attention in places they’ve chosen to spend time by helping them discover relevant content,” says Adam Key, senior marketing manager at Fire TV. “To raise awareness of the Rising Voices initiative, Indeed worked with Amazon Ads to leverage our most impactful and visual placements on the device, including in the feature rotator at the top of the screen.”

The Amazon Ads survey found that 95 percent of U.S. consumers want to decide when and where they interact with a brand. And 80 percent of consumers have a strong desire for more human connection. The Rising Voices program addressed both needs by providing compelling, moving human stories in the short films, which were teased in ad content that engaged viewers where and when they were in the mood to watch. And people did watch, quickly bypassing the goal of 200,000 hours streamed on Fire TV and exceeding it by three times.

“It was incredible,” says Gossfield. “Our faces were everywhere: on the Fire TV home screen, Prime Video, websites, social media — everywhere. Quincy and I have been doing independent filmmaking for so long, but we’d never ever had that kind of ad backing. We got so many phone calls and text messages. We felt seen. It was very, very humbling.”

The best connections are the ones built on shared values. Brands must be genuine with consumers, build trust and demonstrate how they’re committed. In the Amazon Ads survey, 62 percent of U.S. consumers indicate that they’re currently purchasing from brands whose values align with their own. And 80 percent firmly believe they can vote with their dollars by supporting brands that are good corporate citizens. “If you’re honest and authentic when you engage with consumers, you are in a better position to build a lasting relationship with them,” Graves explains. “However, if you lose their trust, it is extremely difficult to regain it.”

The Rising Voices program has followed through on its mission to provide real mentorship and ongoing support for the filmmakers. “We are helping the artists find agents and managers to move their careers forward,” says Rishi Rajani, president of Hillman Grad. “We are also giving them opportunities and working with them on TV episodes and feature films. So many of them are doing so well.”

When brands understand their customers’ needs and values, they can be sure they’re telling the right stories to the right audiences at the right time — and in the right places. With the Rising Voices program and Amazon Ads, Indeed and Hillman Grad were able to tell their brand stories to a broad audience and make a lasting impact by bringing underrepresented stories directly into their homes. And that will continue: Phase two of Rising Voices is already in the works.

“We want to build on top of its success,” Davis says. “I see this program running for years to come.”
